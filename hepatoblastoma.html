Load datasets

Hepatoblastoma is one of the primary liver cancers in young children, with the disease typically manifesting in children under 4 year of age \(^1\). The following analysis uses a hepatoblastoma dataset from a 2021 study in which investigators sought to use single cell RNAseq to identify cell populations and molecular pathways that serve as key drivers of the disease \(^2\). This dataset can be found using the following Gene Expression Omnibus accession number (GSE180666).

Quality control and filtering

merged$mitoPercent <- PercentageFeatureSet(merged, pattern='^MT-')
VlnPlot(merged, features = c("nFeature_RNA", "nCount_RNA", "mitoPercent"), 
        ncol = 3)

Figure 1. Quality control metrics for Feature counts, RNA counts, and percent mitochondrial genes.

After examining the above violin plots, one can see that one of the background samples (HB17_background) contains a high percentage of mitochondrial genes relative to the rest of the samples. Coupled with low features counts, such results could be indicative of a large proportion of dead or damaged cells. The following thresholds will be used to filter the dataset: nCount_RNA > 800 and nFeature_RNA > 500. Since it is normal for cancer cells to have high metabolic rates (and thus higher mitochondrial expression) the mitoPercent < 10 will be the mitochondrial threshold.

# filter dataset based on gene, feature, and mitochondrial gene count
merged_seurat_filtered <- subset(merged, subset = nCount_RNA > 800 &
                                         nFeature_RNA > 500 &
                                         mitoPercent < 10)

After filtering we are left with a total of 49727 cells and 33538 genes.

Data preprocessing

Normalization, scaling and PCA

After performing quality control, a series of of preprocessing steps will need to be implemented. Normalization will transform the raw data so that the expression of each gene is comparable across cells as a proportion of total counts. FindVariableFeatures identifies the top 2000 genes most responsible for the variability in the data, while scaling gives genes across each cell a mean of 0 and a variance of 1. This will prevent highly expressed genes from dominating how cells are clustered relative to each other. Lastly, PCA will conduct dimensionality reduction, generating PCs composed of combinations of genes that are believed to be responsible for most of the variability in the dataset.

# remove seurat objects, since they are no longer needed
rm(cancer.HB17,cancer.HB30,cancer.HB53,normal.HB17,normal.HB53, merged)

# perform standard workflow, normalizing, scaling data, and 
# conducting dimensionality reduction
merged_seurat_filtered <- NormalizeData(merged_seurat_filtered)
merged_seurat_filtered <- FindVariableFeatures(merged_seurat_filtered)
merged_seurat_filtered <- ScaleData(merged_seurat_filtered)
merged_seurat_filtered <- RunPCA(merged_seurat_filtered)

Perform data integration

The integration step is essential for ensuring cell types across treatments groups and batches cluster together. Harmony will be used instead of Seurat’s CCA for its speed and ability to handle batch effects without over-correcting and potentially removing biological signal.

#integrate layers
merged_seurat_filtered <- IntegrateLayers(
  object = merged_seurat_filtered, method = HarmonyIntegration,
  orig.reduction = "pca", new.reduction = "harmony",
  verbose = FALSE
)
# re-join layers after integration
merged_seurat_filtered[["RNA"]] <- JoinLayers(merged_seurat_filtered[["RNA"]])

Manual annotation of cell clusters

Differentially expressed genes that define clusters will be extracted and plugged into PangaoDB, Cellmarker 2.0, and Claude in order to identify the most likely cell types that make each cluster. Cluster 7 and 14 are composed of endothelial cells (EGFL7, and FLT1), cluster 8 and 11 of kupffer/M2 cells (CD163, CSF1R, C1QA, MRC1, MSR1, and MARCH1), cluster 15 of erythrocytes (HBB, HBA1, HBA2, and GYPA), cluster 10 of exhausted memory CD8T cells (CD247, PTPRC, LCK, CCR7, SLAMF6, RUNX3), cluster 9 of hepatic satellite cells (COL1A2, COL3A1, and MYL9), cluster 12 of biliary/cholangiocytes (KRT19, MMP7, and KRT7), cluster 1, 17, and 16 of normal hepatocytes (CYP3A4), and cluster 13 of neurons (NRP2, TGFB2, NRCAM, and NFASC). Clusters 0,4,5,3,6, and 2 make up tumor cells.

Differential gene expression

After identifying major cell types the next step is to perform differential gene expression analysis. Focus will center on the non-tumor cell populations of the dataset. It should be noted that because the normal samples only consist of 2 replicates, pseudo-bulk aggregated methods cannot be used. Wilcoxon will be used instead, with stringent filtering to handle the test’s tendency to produce false positives do to its treatment of each cell as a replicate.

# endothelial
endo.de <- FindMarkers(merged_seurat_filtered, ident.1 = "endothelial_cancer", 
                       ident.2 = "endothelial_normal",test.use = "wilcox", 
                       verbose = FALSE, slot = "data")
endo.de <- endo.de[endo.de$p_val_adj < 0.01 & (endo.de$avg_log2FC > 1.5 |
                           endo.de$avg_log2FC < -1.5), ]
endo.de$gene_names <- rownames(endo.de)
endo.up.genes <- rownames(endo.de)

For endothelial cells, noteworthy upregulated genes include HIF-1 alpha and VEGFC. There is a phenomenon in in Hepatoblastoma known as the Warburg effect. In this situation tumors tend to produce energy via glycolosis rather than oxidative phosphorylation\({^3}\). This acidic environment results in HIF-1 alpha and VEGF production in endothelial cells, promoting angiogenesis. The kupffer cell cluster appears to be composed of cells that are shifting to an M2 macrophage state. Important up-regulated genes in cancer tissue include SPP1 (found to be involved in stemness, chemoresistance, immunosuppression, and angiogenesis \({^4}\)), CSF1 (involved in establishing a tumor associated macrophage (TAM) recruitment loop, M2 polarization\({^5}\), MMP2/MMP14 (involved in extracellular matrix degeneration, invasion, and metastasis \({^6}\), and ANGPT2 (involved in tumor angiogenesis and vascular remodeling\({^7}\)).

Exhausted memory T cells in cancer tissue have high levels of genes expressed involved in exhaustion and immune suppression (IKZF2, ICOS, and SLAMF1\(^{8,9,10}\)), impairing the bodies ability to attack and reverse cancer cell expansion. Stellate cells in cancer tissues were up-regulated in FAP, IL34, ANGPT2, and VCAM1, which play a roles in TAM recruitment/M2 polarization, angiogenesis, immune cell retention and erythroblastic island formation respectively. While there is little evidence to show that stellate cells express these genes, they are elevated in cancer associated fibroblasts. These cells develop from hepatic stellate cells and can support tumorgenesis by stimulating proliferation, angiogenesis and remodeling the micro-environment \(^{11}\). IL-34 has been found to be up-regulated in several cancers, and studies like the one conducted by Franze et al. on colorectal cancer revealed that CAF secreted IL-34 led to the promotion of cancer growth/migration via netrin-1 and basic-fibroblast growth factor \(^{12}\). Similar mechanisms could be at play in hepatoblastoma. Other studies have found ANGPT2 up-regulation driving pro-angiogenic activity \(^{13}\), and VCAM-1 enhancing the growth and invasion of cancer \(^{14}\).
